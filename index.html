Top-line view

We have a solid foundational funnel in the UK. The question is which parts of that funnel scale centrally, which need local investment, and what's the minimum viable resourcing per market.

The core tension: every new market carries linear OPEX (Klaviyo, CW hires, pricing analysts, copywriters). We need each market to hit a critical revenue threshold for the unit economics to pay off. Below that threshold, we're burning cash to build infrastructure we can't yet monetise.

Open question for discussion

What is the minimum monthly revenue per market for OPEX to break even? Working assumption: ~£500k/month. Needs validation with Finance.

Function-by-Function Breakdown

For each function: what can run centrally from London, what must be hired locally per market, and what can be outsourced as a one-time or ongoing external engagement.

1
E-com Product (Website)
Critical path

The website is the bottleneck. Nothing else works until the localised site is live - PM can't run feeds, pricing can't set up SKUs, CRM can't build flows, CW can't service customers. This is the gate.

Central

  • Arman oversees rollout (20% of time per market)
  • Keep sites simple until £1-1.5M/month
  • Template from Germany launch - each subsequent market should be faster

Local hire

  • None initially - centralised PM capacity

External / hire

  • Front-end engineer (full-time) - Seb replacement. This is the single most critical hire. Dedicated to localising and maintaining international domains
  • Additional PM capacity once any market hits £1-1.5M/month
Key risk

Without a dedicated front-end resource, website localisation timelines slip. Every week of delay pushes back PM launch, pricing setup, CRM flows, and CW readiness for that market. The front-end engineer is the single highest-leverage hire in this plan.

2
Performance Marketing (Paid Search)

Paid Search is the primary acquisition channel internationally. Paid Social is deprioritised until a market hits ~£1M/month, at which point we may consider localised social.

Central

  • Leo's team + Trending Trades manage campaigns
  • Campaign structure built per cluster (not per market). DACH runs one structure with different pricing for AT/CH
  • Budget management scales easily - not a constraint

Local hire

  • None needed initially

External

  • Feed localisation - the critical dependency. Two options:
  • Feedonomics: charges per feed. Costs multiply with each market. Proven but expensive at scale
  • Tides: flat fee. Must keep them engaged for ongoing markets. More cost-effective at 4+ clusters
  • Local keyword research per language cluster
  • Translated ad copy per language cluster
Scaling advantage

PM scales relatively well centrally. Campaign structures are per-cluster (not per-country), so DACH is one structure. The main cost driver is feed localisation - the Feedonomics vs Tides decision becomes material at 4 clusters.

3
Pricing
Scaling risk

Pricing is the function with the biggest scaling risk. The current process is analyst-intensive and doesn't have robust automation yet.

Central

  • Anton leads DE pricing; Vinayak backfills UK
  • Akash continues scraper investment
  • Incoming data science hire to build intelligence layer (margin maximisation without losing conversion)
  • Central pricing framework / methodology

Local hire

  • 0.5-1 pricing analyst per cluster (depends on competitor overlap)
  • ~1 analyst per 500 SKUs as a rough ratio

External

  • Scraper scaling (Akash / data science)
Key risk

At 4 clusters x 500+ SKUs each, the analyst headcount scales linearly (2-4 additional analysts). The competitive landscape is unknown for most markets - we don't yet know how much overlap there is between competitor sets. If competitors differ significantly per market, the analytical lift is higher. The data science hire and scraper investment are the path to bending this cost curve.

Open question

How different is the competitor landscape market by market? Common comparators (iHerb, Amazon) likely exist everywhere, but local players may change the game. Needs market-by-market competitor mapping before we can right-size the analyst team.

4
Retention / CRM

CRM flows are the connective tissue. Having done Germany first, subsequent markets should be significantly easier - the templates, logic, and curation approach carry over. The main gap is localised copy.

Central

  • Holly leads flow setup, supported by agency
  • Germany template becomes the playbook - future markets are faster
  • Curation angle must be consistent across markets
  • Recharge setup per market (requires dev resource)

Local hire

  • Local copywriter per language cluster - this is the big gap. Not just translation but culturally adapted copy
  • Website localisation copy (shared with Product)

External

  • Klaviyo account per market (linear OPEX cost)
  • Agency support on flow builds
  • AI translation accelerates but doesn't replace native review
Scaling advantage

Germany is the hard one. Once the CRM architecture and flow templates are proven, each subsequent market is primarily a copywriting + localisation exercise. The strategic decisions (flow logic, timing, offers) are made once centrally.

5
Customer Wellbeing

CW is non-negotiable - it's a core differentiator. Every language cluster needs a lead who can provide the Healf-quality customer experience in the local language. This scales linearly with markets.

Central

  • Gorgias integrations per market (straightforward, need monitoring)
  • Central CW playbook and quality standards
  • Firas / Harriet set standards and train

Local hire

  • 1 CW lead per language cluster
  • Must be native-level in the market language
  • Scale capacity within each cluster as volume grows

External

  • AI translation can handle tier-1 queries initially
  • But wellbeing advice needs human, native-language delivery
Key constraint

CW hires are the hardest to compress. You can't centralise wellbeing advice across languages - customers need to feel understood in their language. Each new language cluster = at least one new hire. This is irreducible linear OPEX.

What Scales Centrally vs. What Scales Linearly

This is the key insight for the discussion. Some functions get cheaper per-market as we add markets. Others cost the same every time.

Gating decision

Do we launch clusters sequentially (lower risk, slower) or overlap them (faster, but front-end engineer becomes the bottleneck)? With one front-end engineer, we can realistically launch 1 new cluster every 4-6 weeks. Overlapping requires either a second engineer or accepting slower per-market quality.
