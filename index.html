Key Consideration

Every new market carries a fixed amount of OPEX: Klaviyo account, TripleWhale account, Feedonomics feed, pricing analyst capacity, CW hires, and copywriter resource. We need to make sure each market hits a critical revenue threshold to justify this fixed investment.

The exact threshold needs validation with Finance, but the principle is clear: we should not turn on a market unless we have confidence it can reach the revenue level that covers its per-market cost base within a reasonable timeframe.

Function-by-Function Breakdown

For each function: what can run centrally from London, what must be hired locally per market, and what external providers or additional hires are needed.

1
E-com Product (Website)
Critical path

The website is the bottleneck. Nothing else can be actioned until the localised website is live: PM can't run feeds, pricing can't set up SKUs, CRM can't build flows, CW can't service customers. The localised site unlocks everything else - feed and PM campaign setup, SKU and pricing setup, CRM flows, and CW - all of which can then happen in parallel.

Central

  • Arman spends 20-25% of his capacity overseeing international markets
  • Keep sites very simple, similar to Germany
  • Translate from German for DACH-adjacent markets
  • Each subsequent market will be broadly linear effort

Local hire

  • None - centralised PM capacity

Additional hire

  • Phil (front-end engineer, replacing Seb) - will spend the majority of his time getting localised websites live
  • Current estimate: 3-4 weeks per localisation, can potentially be significantly fast-tracked with a great dev
  • Around EOY, may need additional PM capacity if we want to start running optimisations on international domains
Key risk

Making sure Phil is onboarded quickly and is a killer. He is the single most critical hire in this plan. Website localisation gates every other function for every market.

2
Performance Marketing (Paid Search)

Paid Search is the primary acquisition channel internationally. The same set of things need to be replicated across the five markets: Google account setup, campaign setup, feed optimisation, and TripleWhale tracking. This can all be managed centrally.

Central

  • Leo manages centrally
  • Trending Tides manages campaign setup - within a campaign setup we can run multiple prices across markets
  • Additional hire: 1 dedicated PM EU Manager, reports to / works with Leo, provides central capacity to manage PPC across five markets

Local hire

  • None

External

  • Google - account setup directly managed by Google
  • Feedonomics - feed localisation per market
  • TripleWhale - additional account for tracking across every localised website
  • Tides - flat fee, sequential rollout across campaigns
  • Tides will also lead deeper engagement with Google to set up accounts per market
Scaling advantage

Scales well centrally. The same set of things (Google account setup, campaign setup, feed optimisation, TripleWhale tracking) need to be replicated across the five markets. All spend can be managed by one central PM EU Manager alongside Leo.

Paid Social is deprioritised until a market hits a critical threshold (~£1-1.5M/month), at which point we may consider setting up a localised paid social operation.

3
Pricing

The current pricing process is analyst-intensive and highly manual.

Central

  • Anton leads the pricing function
  • Critical that Akash continues to build and improve the pricing scraper
  • Marginal effort to add additional URLs for different markets is low
  • Currency and interface handling may require slight differences to make execution easy for each analyst

Local hire

  • 0.5-1 pricing analyst per cluster depending on competitive overlap and number of SKUs
  • One analyst can roughly cover 500-700 SKUs (depends on order mix)
  • Currently for DACH: 0.5 analyst capacity initially

Additional hire

  • AI / Data Scientist - to scale pricing experiments across multiple markets and optimise margin
  • This is distinct from the price-match scraper that Akash is building
Key headcount risk

We'll need another 2-3 analysts to scale across all clusters. A big factor is the competitive overlap between markets - we don't currently have visibility on this. The data science hire and scraper investment are the paths to bending this cost curve and ensuring accuracy of pricing updates.

Open question 1

How different are the competitive landscapes per market? This drives how many analysts we need and how much work can be shared across markets.

Open question 2

How should we structure the pricing team? Two options: one analyst per brand cluster, across multiple markets (deep brand expertise) or one analyst per market, across multiple brands (deep market expertise). Leaning towards deep market expertise. The answer depends on competitive overlap - which we assume is very high for the SKU portfolio. In an ideal world, we have EU-level analysts who manage the SKU portfolio across multiple markets and multiple competitors at an EU level, because the portfolio will dictate the commonality. If we have a common portfolio across all of EU being served from the same warehouse, it likely makes sense to have one analyst managing multiple brands across the EU market cluster.

4
Retention / CRM

Copy translation is the critical lift here. The copy needs to be fully localised - this is a one-time effort per market but also creates a brake every time we want to edit a flow. The advantage is that the curation angle is similar across markets, so all we need is translation effort rather than a rethink of the curation strategy.

Central

  • Yocto leads flow setup and account warming for localised websites
  • Holly oversees centrally
  • Future markets will be faster now that we have done this for Germany
  • Curation angle is consistent across markets - only translation needed, not curation effort

Local hire

  • 1 local copywriter per language cluster - the critical gap

External

  • Klaviyo - separate account per market
  • Yocto leads flow setup and account warming per market
  • Recharge - separate account per market
Scaling advantage

Germany is the hard one. Now that the CRM architecture and flow templates are proven, each subsequent market is primarily a copywriting and translation exercise. The strategic decisions (flow logic, timing, offers, curation) are made once centrally.

5
Customer Wellbeing

CW is a core differentiator for Healf. Every language cluster needs quality service. There are two models to consider: a low-touch AI-assisted approach, or a fully localised intensive approach.

Low-touch model: Outside of Germany, use AI translation to handle customer queries. The warehouse setup is similar across markets, so AI can effectively deflect a large share of standard queries. Lower cost, faster to scale.

Intensive model: Full native-language CW support per market. Delivers the Healf-quality experience but carries significantly higher OPEX.

Central

  • Gorgias integration per market
  • Central CW playbook and policies
  • Firas / Harriet set standards and provide training resources
  • Demetra supports training

Local hire (intensive model)

  • 1-2 CW hires per language cluster
  • Must have native-level proficiency in both English and local language
  • Scale capacity as business grows in each market

Considerations

  • As a % of revenue, CW costs may be higher if resource sits onshore vs. offshore
  • Hiring native speakers in traditional offshore centres (e.g. Philippines) will be challenging
  • Can explore other regions - potentially South America or Eastern Europe - where language speakers may be available at lower cost
Decision required

Which CW model do we invest in? Low-touch with AI is cheaper and faster. Intensive is the Healf-quality experience. Could also stage it: low-touch at launch, upgrade to intensive once a market proves out.
